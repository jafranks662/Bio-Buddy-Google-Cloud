# Question 1
A student uses the following method to study the movement of water molecules across a semipermeable material:
1. Prepare five salt water solutions (0.2 g/L, 0.4 g/L, 0.6 g/L, 0.8 g/L, 1.0 g/L).
2. Place 10 mL of the 0.6 g/L solution in five semipermeable material pouches and seal the tops.
3. Place 150 mL of each solution in separate beakers. Attach a single pouch with the 0.6 g/L solution to the side of each beaker.
4. Wait for 30 minutes.

In which beaker solution environment would the most water movement INTO the pouch occur?

A) 0.2 g/L solution beaker
B) 0.4 g/L solution beaker
C) 0.6 g/L solution beaker
D) 1.0 g/L solution beaker

Correct: A
Explanation: Water moves from areas of lower solute concentration to areas of higher solute concentration by osmosis. The 0.2 g/L beaker has a lower salt concentration than the 0.6 g/L pouch, so water will move into the pouch where the salt concentration is higher.

# Question 3
A student uses the following method to study the movement of water molecules across a semipermeable material:
1. Prepare five salt water solutions (0.2 g/L, 0.4 g/L, 0.6 g/L, 0.8 g/L, 1.0 g/L).
2. Place 10 mL of the 0.6 g/L solution in five semipermeable material pouches and seal the tops.
3. Place 150 mL of each solution in separate beakers. Attach a single pouch with the 0.6 g/L solution to the side of each beaker.
4. Wait for 30 minutes.

In which beaker solution environment would there be NO NET movement of water (equilibrium)?

A) 0.2 g/L solution beaker
B) 0.4 g/L solution beaker
C) 0.6 g/L solution beaker
D) 1.0 g/L solution beaker

Correct: C
Explanation: When the solute concentration is equal on both sides of a semipermeable membrane (0.6 g/L inside the pouch and 0.6 g/L in the beaker), the system is at equilibrium. Water molecules still move in both directions, but there is no net movement in either direction.

# Question 4
A cell has a higher solute concentration than its surrounding beaker solution. What will happen to water movement?

A) Water moves into the cell
B) Water moves out of the cell
C) Water moves into and out of the cell equally
D) No water movement occurs

Correct: A
Explanation: When a cell has a higher solute concentration than its surroundings, water will move by osmosis from the area of lower solute concentration (outside the cell) to the area of higher solute concentration (inside the cell). This causes the cell to swell.

# Question 5
A cell has the same solute concentration as its surrounding beaker solution. What will happen to water movement?

A) Water moves into the cell
B) Water moves out of the cell
C) Water moves into and out of the cell equally
D) No water movement occurs

Correct: C
Explanation: When the solute concentration is equal inside and outside the cell, the cell is in an isotonic solution. Water molecules move across the membrane in both directions at equal rates, resulting in no net change in cell volume.

A) Water moves into the cell

Correct: B
Explanation: When a cell has a lower solute concentration than its surroundings, water will move by osmosis from inside the cell (lower solute concentration) to outside the cell (higher solute concentration). This causes the cell to shrink.

# Question 8
An incomplete diagram of a cell in a saline solution is shown. The cell has a low salt concentration and the surrounding saline solution has a high salt concentration. This type of water movement is identified as which process?

A) Active transport
B) Endocytosis
C) Diffusion
D) Osmosis

Correct: D
Explanation: Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a semipermeable membrane from an area of lower solute concentration to an area of higher solute concentration. This is a passive process that does not require energy.

# Question 11
A second experiment involves placing potato slices in sugar water. Which result should be expected if the cell membranes of potato slices are semipermeable?

A) Neither sugar nor water moving from one side of the membrane to the other
B) Only water moving from one side of the membrane to the other
C) Only sugar moving from one side of the membrane to the other
D) Both sugar and water moving from one side of the membrane to the other

Correct: B
Explanation: Semipermeable membranes are selectively permeable, meaning they allow some molecules to pass through but not others. Cell membranes are permeable to small water molecules but not to larger sugar molecules, so only water would move across the membrane by osmosis.
